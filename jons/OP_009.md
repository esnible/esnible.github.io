# A TRIAL LISTING OF THE MODERN GOLD COINS OF OTTOMAN EGYPT

# BY SAMUEL LACHMAN

# INTRODUCTION

This is an attempt to list the main varieties known to exist of the gold coins struck in Ottoman Egypt after the coinage reform of Muhammad Ali in A.H. 1251/1834 A.D. The reform began with an ordinance of the same year (1), and the pertinent particulars were that the gold was to be 875/1000 fine, the following denominations being provided for:-

| 500 piastres | 37 mm | 42.85 g | (5 livres Egyptiennes) |
|:--- |:--- |:--- |:--- |
| 100 piastres | 21.4 mm | 8.544 g | (1 livre égyptienne) |
| 50 piastres | 18.0 mm | 4.274 g | (½ livre égyptienne) |
| 25 piastres | mm | 2.136 g | (¼ livre égyptienne) |
| 20 piastres | mm | 1.7088 g | (Masriah) |
| 10 piastres | mm | 0.8544 g | (Nousf Masriah) |
| 5 piastres | 12.9 mm | 0.4272 g | (Rebiah Masriah) |

Diameters seem to vary slightly and the 100 piastre coin of the 12th regnal year of Abdul Hamid II has a diameter of 23.9 mm. Production commenced in 1836 but in no reign were all denominations minted, the 50 piastre piece being replaced at some point by one of 25 Piastres,

All coins bear on the obverse the tughra of the Ottoman sultan. In the reign of Mahmud II the word (adli = just) appears at the right of the tughra being replaced in subsequent issues by a flower or on some of the later coins of Abdul Hamid II by the word (Al-Ghazi). Below the tughra is a number indicating the denomination in piastres and the Arabic letter (sh for ghurush). The field of both sides is otherwise plain, with the exception of the 100 piastre coin of the 12th year of Abdul Hamid II, which has floral borders on both sides. The tughra of the sultans is identical with that on the coins struck at Istanbul as described by Cüneyt Ölçer (2), with the exception of the tughra of Murad V which reads 'Khan Murad b. Abdul Mejid elmuzaffer daima' (instead of Mehmed Murad) (3). The reverse inscription reads:- duriba fi misr (struck in Egypt). All coins have a reeded edge.

The coins were all struck at Cairo up to the 8th regnal year of Abdul Hamid II (1883) when the mint was closed. Egyptian coinage was changed again in 1303/1885, after which only one gold coin was produced for circulation. The 100 piastre pieces of 1888 were struck at the official Berlin mint, the mintage being quoted as 52,024 pieces (4). 10 and 5 piastre coins of later dates were struck as presentation pieces or as coin jewellery for private orders outside Egypt (5). No details have come to light but they were probably produced in private mints.
At no time during the period were adequate quantities of gold coins minted. At the time of Muhammad Ali's reform, numerous foreign gold coins were in circulation. The ordinance of 1251/1834 fixed the following rates of exchange (P.T. = piastre tarif) (9). British Sovereign 97 P.T. French Louis d'or 77 6/40 P.T. Zequin (Turkish fundukli altin) 45 26/40 P.T. Boundoukli (Venetian ducat) 46 17/40 P.T. Doublon (Spanish) 313 29/40 P.T.

After the coinage reform of 1303/1885, the British sovereign, the French Napoleon d'or and the Turkish gold pound were declared legal tender in Egypt at rates of 97.50, 77.15 and 87.75 Egyptian piastres respectively. In addition, the Egyptian government began to withdraw Egyptian gold coins from circulation (10).

Some pattern coins, engraved by Albert Borve (11), were struck at the Paris mint dated regnal year 4. The coins do not show the flower near the tughra and the edge is plain. No such coins were minted for circulation. The particulars are as follows (12):-

| 400 piastres | 38 mm | 34 g |
|:--- |:--- |:--- |
| 200 piastres | 30 mm | 17 g |
| 100 piastres | 21 mm | 8.50 g |
| 50 piastres | 19 mm | 4.25 g |
| 25 piastres | 17 mm | 2.10 g |

The 400 piastre is illustrated in the catalogue of the Egypt Palace collection, lot No. 1257.

# MINTAGES

The following table lists the numbers of the gold coins struck between 1836 and 1853 as recorded by Ahmad Fuad Pasha (6). The calendar period corresponding to each year is unknown. Before the French invasion of 1798, the financial year began at the end of August, but this may well have been changed. The coins struck in a certain year cannot be assumed to bear the corresponding regnal year. This could be true in some cases, but coins may have been struck in advance or at a later date. The mintage figures give therefore only a general idea of the quantities which were minted; for example, the listing of 56 5 livre pieces for 1875 seems to be erroneous. These coins were probably minted in 1876.

In order to facilitate comparisons, the mintage figures are supplemented by lists showing the dates on which each regnal year began. They have been calculated from the Muslim dates mentioned by Cüneyt Ölçer (7), with the help of tables by G.S.P. Freeman-Grenville (8). Various other sources give different dates and in view of the fact that the Muslim day begins in the evening, differences of a day are possible.
